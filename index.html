Research

My research focuses on problems related to climate change mitigation, such as optimizing flexible loads, enabling electric vehicle participation in power grids, and managing local energy markets, all geared towards increasing the proportion of renewable energy on the grid.
I use optimization, machine learning and controls methods in my research, and I am also interested in game theoretic and statistics methods. A summary of my PhD research is given below, and a complete list of my publications can be found on Google Scholar.

Mobile Storage (Electric Vehicle Batteries) in Power Networks

Electric vehicles batteries can provide valuable services to the grid, for example: by charging when energy is cheap/clean, and discharging when a replacement for polluting/expensive generation sources is needed. We analyze the value of mobile energy storage such as EVs in the power network, and optimize their participation in electricity markets. We also analyze the equilibrium behaviour of a fleet of independently owned EVs who participate in the power network.

Optimizing Flexible Load Participation in Electricity Markets (Demand Response)

Flexible energy consumption can be a valuable resource for the power grid, particularly in emergency situations where it can help avoid the use of polluting and expensive peaker plants. We model flexible load capabilities using a probabilistic framework, and optimize flexible load participation in utility programs and electricity markets. We examine the effects of aggregating assets in this setup, and develop a complementarity test to decide which assets will form good aggregations.
We are now using commercial demand response data to forecast flexible load in a probabilistic manner using machine learning methods. The key challenge is that demand response events are rare, and we have sparse performance data. At the same time, forecasting this flexibility accurately is critical to ensure safe operation of the power grid.

Optimal Prosumer Aggregations: Design, Modeling, Pricing

Smart buildings equipped with solar rooftops, backup batteries, electric vehicle chargers and smart HVAC systems can help provide valuable services to the grid by functioning as prosumers, i.e., by producing as well as consuming energy. We study the formation and operation of local energy markets, where prosumers can trade their surplus energy with each other. We develop metrics for optimal aggregations, i.e., which prosumers would benefit mutually when placed in the same market.

Electrifying Long Haul Truck Freight: Technology and Impacts

Electrifying long haul freight is an essential step in reducing carbon emissions. Technological innovations and infrastructure outlay can enable the electrification of long haul truck trips. We estimate the needs (technology and infrastructure) for electrification and the impacts on the electric grid. We develop a prioritization scheme of resources to achieve widespread electrification through a data driven optimization model. This analysis fed into the 2035 Report 2.0 by UC Berkeley, which helped inform policymakers and mapped realistic decarbonization roadmaps for 2035.
